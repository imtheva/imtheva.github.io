The aim of the present work was to investigate the principle of non-Newtonian capillary rheometry for quality control purposes. In the investigation, three series of acrylic-based viscoelastic coating samples with different types and concentrations of pigments and thickening agents were used, and the rheological measurements were compared to results obtained with the so-called Stormer viscometer and an advanced off-line rheometer. A detailed analysis of the potential measurement implications was also conducted. For shear stresses from 15.0 to 350.0 Pa (the upper boundary), the novel capillary rheometer was found to provide results in good quantitative agreement with the advanced rheometer when sample holding time, and thereby shear history, was properly controlled. The resolution of the capillary rheometer was sufficiently high to allow detection of the rheology changes associated with variations in coating formulations of pigment volume and rheology modifier concentrations. Hypertension is a leading preventable risk factor for cardiovascular disease and premature deaths. Hypertension and anti-hypertensive have been associated with decreased semen volume, sperm count, motility, abnormal sperm morphology and erectile dysfunction. Consequently, hypertension and anti-hypertensives contribute to male infertility. Male infertility contributes over 50% of the cases of infertility amongst couples and studies have shown that male infertility increases with 0.3% annually. Methods This is an integrative literature review on published articles. Purposeful sampling was used. Relevant data from PubMed, Medline, Google scholar, Cochrane and ResearchGate was searched, evaluated for its relevance and reviewed. Further, grey literature was reviewed and the search was done in English. Owing to the scarcity of the research data in this field, the search went as far back as 20 years ago. Results The search strategy yielded a grand total of 99,434 electronic citations from the years 2003 to 2023, a period of 20 years. 112 study papers were included and scrutinized in the integrative literature review and thematic data analysis was done on effects of hypertension and the antihypertensive drugs on male fertility. Conclusion Men with hypertension have a higher risk of experiencing fertility problems than men without hypertension. Studies have also found that the use of certain antihypertensive medications negatively affects sperm quality, volume and motility and consequently, male fertility. Antihypertensive medication in hypertensive men should be based on the individual’s overall health and his reproductive health needs.
